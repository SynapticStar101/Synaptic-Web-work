Day Zero: What Have I Done?

The origin story. Where it all began. Where I had absolutely no idea what I was getting into.

Let me paint you a picture. It's a Tuesday evening. I'm sitting at my desk, mildly bored, mildly over-caffeinated, and mildly convinced that I, a person who has never written a line of code in their professional life, should make a video game.

Reasonable? No. Possible? Well, that's where it gets interesting.

The Dangerous Question

I had been hearing a lot about "vibe coding" — this idea that you could describe what you wanted to build to an AI and it would help you actually build it. Not just explain concepts. Not just write documentation. Actually build the thing.

So I opened Claude and typed, with all the casual confidence of someone who has absolutely no idea what they're about to get themselves into:

"Hey, can you help me make a retro endless runner type game but with bins? Like where you're dodging and collecting wheely bins?"

There was a pause. (There wasn't, really — Claude replied instantly. But in my memory, there was a pause, and somewhere in a server farm, a very patient AI took a deep breath.)

Then it said yes. And then everything changed.

Step One: The Part Where I Discovered I Needed Things I Didn't Have

Claude started explaining what we'd need. A code editor (VSCode, apparently — "it's free and excellent"). Node.js (what?). Git (come again?). A GitHub account (okay I think I'd heard of that).

I had none of these things. I downloaded all of them within about 15 minutes, understanding approximately none of what I was installing or why. The terminal opened for the first time and I genuinely felt like I'd accidentally accessed the Matrix.

💡
Non-dev tip: The terminal is not as scary as it looks. It's just a very minimalist way to tell your computer what to do. You will type things in it and feel extremely powerful. Until you break something. Then you will feel extremely small.

Step Two: The First Line of Code

Claude walked me through creating a project folder, initialising it (I still don't fully know what this means), and creating the first file. An HTML file. A canvas. A spaceship made of a single triangle.

I hit save, opened the file in my browser, and there it was. A tiny triangle on a black background.

Reader, I stared at that triangle for a full minute. That triangle was mine. I had made that triangle happen. I, a person who last year thought JavaScript was something to do with coffee, had caused a triangle to exist on a screen.

Step Three: Chaos, Momentum, and the Git Incident

Things progressed. The lanes appeared. Barney started running. Bins scrolled toward him from the right. I hit Enter and he honked. I didn't know why honking was a feature. I still don't fully know. But it is absolutely the best part of the game, and I will die on that hill.

Then I tried to "save my work to Git" as Claude suggested. I didn't really understand what Git was. I clicked some things in VSCode. I typed some commands in the terminal. I pushed something somewhere.

I still don't fully know what I pushed or where it went. But it went somewhere, and the next day it was still there, and I'm calling that a win.

⚠
Genuine warning: If you're a non-dev starting this journey — Git will confuse you. It confused me. It will feel like the computer is mad at you personally. It is not. It is just Git.

What I Learned on Day Zero

  • VSCode is your friend. Learn the keyboard shortcuts. It will feel like a superpower.
  • The terminal is not your enemy. It just looks like one.
  • Claude is an extraordinarily patient collaborator who will explain the same thing seventeen different ways without ever sighing at you.
  • There is an enormous difference between understanding code and being able to make stuff with code. You can do the latter without much of the former. (Don't tell the developers.)
  • The feeling of seeing something you imagined actually work on a screen is addictive. Like, properly addictive. I understand now why people become developers.

What Comes Next

Barney's Bins was just the beginning. After that came BinDaRoids. After that came this website. And somewhere in all of it, I accidentally learned things. Not enough to call myself a developer. But enough to be dangerous.

Follow along. It gets weirder.

— Barney, Non-Dev, Accidental Game Creator
