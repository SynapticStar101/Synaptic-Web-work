A Nod to the Coders

To everyone who learned the hard way, from first steps to guru status — this one's for you.

A Massive Nod

This is not a build journal. There's no iteration table. No bugs documented. No lessons about invisible Unicode characters or iOS keyboard security rules.

This is something different. This is a note.

A massive nod to all those that use their hard learned lessons in code — from their first steps to their Guru like status — in bringing to life their own and other people's creative visions.

Because after three games and a website built entirely through vibe coding, I've earned just enough perspective to understand what I haven't been doing.

What I Got a Tiny Insight Into

This exercise in vibe coding has given me a tiny insight into how much stellar effort goes in from them on every thought turned into a line of executable amazingness.

Every thought. Into a line. That executes. That does what the thought said it should do. That works on a phone, and a laptop, and a browser three versions old, and on iOS with its particular opinions about keyboard focus.

I described things. An AI built them. And even that — just the describing, the testing, the noticing when something wasn't right and trying to explain why — took hours. Days. Dozens of sessions.

Real developers don't have the AI doing the building. They are the AI. They are the one translating the thought into the line of executable amazingness, in their own head, with their own hands.

⭐
For context: I've spent weeks building three retro browser games without writing a single line of code myself. And I'm still in awe of what it takes. Which tells you everything about what it actually takes.

The Tenacity

I bow to their tenacity when having to iterate through line upon line of code to find out exactly where it has tripped over and to pick it up and make it better.

I've seen what that looks like from the outside. Five bugs wearing one coat. A single invisible character causing a full blackout. A platform security rule three layers deep in iOS that you'd only know about if someone had been there before you and left breadcrumbs.

In my case, a tool found those things. A developer finds them with their own eyes, their own reasoning, their own accumulated library of "I've seen this before." That's not a job skill. That's years of earned instinct.

The iteration — the checking, the hunting, the hypothesis and the test and the wrong answer and the try again — that's the job. Not the glamorous bit. The actual job. And they do it, consistently, for things far more complex than a Space Invaders clone with rubbish bins.

What the Games Taught Me About People

When testing these co-created games it has humbled me to know that what was highlighted and fixed here by a programming tool, people who live and breathe this would have already known and pre-empted — making the first alpha release so much better from the get go.

That's the bit that really landed.

The things that took me sessions to find and fix — the ghost input, the viewport height calculation, the CORS wrinkle, the duplicate property name — a developer who has seen those things before walks into the project already knowing. They pre-empt. They build the guard before they need it. They make the first version better than my final version, before they've even started testing.

That's not magic. That's earned knowledge. Lessons learned the hard way, carried forward into every project that comes after.

💡
The difference: I found the bugs. They would have prevented them. That gap — between finding and preventing — is what years of craft looks like.

From First Steps to Guru Status

Every developer was once someone who didn't know what a commit was. Who stared at an error message that made no sense. Who spent three hours on something that turned out to be a missing semicolon. Who had that first moment where something worked, properly, unexpectedly, and felt the specific electricity of it.

From there to guru — from first steps to the person who knows why the iOS keyboard won't open without a direct gesture — that journey is extraordinary. It's not a credential. It's a collection of scars and breakthroughs and late nights and "I've seen this before" moments, accumulated over years of showing up and figuring it out.

I've been given the briefest, most superficial glimpse of that world. I've stood at the edge of it and looked in. And what I saw was people doing something genuinely remarkable, every day, for other people's ideas.

So This Is My Note

To the developers who built the tools I used without knowing how to use them. To the ones who wrote the documentation I searched without fully understanding. To the ones whose Stack Overflow answers from 2014 are still solving problems in 2026.

To the junior devs grinding through their first year. To the seniors keeping ancient codebases alive with sheer willpower. To the ones who spotted the bug in someone else's PR before it hit production. To the open source contributors doing it for nothing but the love of the craft.

I made three games and a website with vibes and AI. You are the reason any of that was possible — because the tools, the languages, the platforms, the browsers, the APIs, all of it was built by people who actually know what they're doing.

This is a massive nod. You have earned it.

— Barney, Non-Dev, Deeply Humbled Arcade Operator
